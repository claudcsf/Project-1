Madeira

Well-known for being one of the regions with the highest density of luxury hotels in Europe, the pearl of the Atlantic hides an enormous potential as an adventure destination. From cliff to cliff, we explored the Highlands that divide the island, landed on the beach on a paraglider and dived in the maritime reservation of Garajau.

Levadas

When the first Portuguese settlers arrived on the island, they were forced to build aqueducts that would carry water from the Northern part of the island to the South, which is traditionally warmer and more fertile. Those water courses - called levadas - have become paths that take us right to the heart of the biggest relic of Laurel forest in the World.

The landscape that surrounds us in those paths remains almost unchanged by mankind. As we cut through the evergreen forest, waterfalls, lakes and sceneries of extraordinary natural beauty appear one after the other. We couldn’t possibly feel further away from the tourist resort, the agitation of the city or from urban stress.

It’s in these waterfalls and water streams that we can try yet another sport - canyoning. Like the levadas themselves, canyoning in Madeira is divided according to areas of greater or lesser difficulty. For those who never tried it, Ribeira das Cales or Ribeiro Frio are the best options. For those who want to challenge themselves, Ribeira do Vimeiro and Ribeira da Hortelã are in for a fight.

When you go through Ribeiro Frio, don’t forget to try a good Madeiran poncha!

Outside of the highlands, it’s worth taking the trail at Ponta de S. Lourenço, in the Southeast part of the island. A local microclimate creates an arid landscape similar to that of Porto Santo, the second island of the archipelago.
