Porto

Porto is a self-made city whose charm lies in its balconies, cobblestone alleyways and its resident’s persistent joie de vivre. You’ll quickly find they are resilient, willing to help anyone despite language barriers and incredibly proud of their city. To give you a little historical context: Porto resisted Napoleonic wars in the early 19th century, fought the liberal wars in Portugal immediately afterwards and grew to have a large industrial fabric by the end of the century. Workers often lived in poor conditions, in back-to-back houses that still exist all over the city.

The hills continue

So Porto certainly does not have Lisbon’s cosmopolitan flair, but residential buildings are colorful, with balconies facing the streets and often decorated with tiles. And after a few decades of stagnation and living behind the times, Porto is now enjoying a renaissance and profound urban rehabilitation plans. You should reserve two or three days to visit – the city center is small and quite walkable. There’s just one little thing: like Lisbon, it’s quite hilly. We hope you’re prepared to walk a bit more!

If you have more time on your hands, we recommend a few day trips to other locations in the North of the country. Venture into the luscious Douro region by boat and find out how Port Wine is made from the grape to your glass or take the train to Guimarães, the “cradle of the country”. Enjoy an authentic side of Portugal and allow time to sit still during the journey.

Start at Cedofeita – this is the old bourgeois quarter, where well to do industrial families built their houses once the city began to grow. Most houses have 3 or 4 floors, with balconies facing the street, which is largely commercial. There’s plenty of street art in this area, and Miguel Bombarda is full of art galleries that premiere new shows every month on the same day – which got it the nickname of Porto’s Art District.
